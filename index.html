Why this site exists

This short site collects core criticisms of contemporary capitalism — ecological overshoot, growing inequality, threats to democratic institutions, and precarious work. It aims to be a primer you can share with friends, adapt for a zine, or use as a starting point for activism.

Core Arguments

Ecological overshoot

Capitalism's growth imperative pushes extraction and consumption beyond planetary limits: endless production + profit-seeking incentivizes short-term resource exploitation and externalizes environmental costs.

Concentrated wealth & inequality

Wealth increasingly concentrates among a tiny elite. This shifts political power, reduces social mobility, and undermines public institutions that serve the majority.

Undermining democracy

When corporations and wealthy actors can capture regulators, elections, and media, policy reflects private profit, not public good — eroding democratic accountability.

Precarious labour

Competition to cut costs drives gigification, wage stagnation, and weakened labour protections — transforming work into insecurity rather than dignified livelihoods.

History of Capitalism

Capitalism emerged in Europe between the 16th and 18th centuries, growing from mercantilist trade and early colonial expansion. The Industrial Revolution dramatically accelerated factory production, urbanization, and wage labour, marking capitalism’s modern form.

By the late 19th century, corporate consolidation and global imperial systems helped concentrate wealth and resources into industrial powers. The 20th century saw major critiques and alternatives, including socialism, communism, and social democracy, leading to labour protections and welfare states in many nations.

Since the 1980s, deregulation, privatization, and financialization—often described as neoliberalism—have reshaped global markets, reducing state control and expanding the influence of corporations and financial actors worldwide. Critics argue that this era intensified inequality, weakened worker protections, and accelerated ecological degradation.

What you can do

Use this primer to start conversations. Host a reading group, share the zine, or post a thread highlighting one argument at a time.

Organize

Support unions, community co-ops, and campaigns pushing for stronger labour laws, corporate transparency, and wealth taxes.
